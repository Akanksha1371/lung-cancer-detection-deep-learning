Normal Lung Tissue

Represents healthy, non-cancerous scan results.

What this classification means

A "Normal" classification indicates that the AI model did not detect significant signs of nodules, masses, or other abnormalities associated with cancer. The lung tissue (parenchyma) appears clear and properly aerated, consistent with a healthy individual.

CT Scan Features

  • Lung Parenchyma: Appears dark (black), indicating it is properly filled with air.
  • Blood Vessels: Visible as a normal, branching tree of white structures.
  • Airways (Bronchi): Appear as dark tubes with thin, barely visible walls.
  • Absence of Abnormalities: No "ground-glass" opacities, no solid masses (nodules), and no enlarged lymph nodes.
{% elif class_name == 'benign' %}

Benign Cases

Non-cancerous nodules or tumors.

What this classification means

A benign nodule is an abnormal growth in the lung that is **not cancerous**. It cannot spread (metastasize) to other parts of the body. Common examples include granulomas (scars from a past infection like tuberculosis) or hamartomas (a disorganized growth of normal lung tissue).

Symptoms

Benign nodules almost never cause symptoms. They are typically asymptomatic and are found by accident on a chest X-ray or CT scan performed for an unrelated reason (e.g., after an injury).

Diagnosis

Radiologists diagnose a nodule as likely benign by observing its characteristics on a CT scan:

  • Size: Usually small (less than 8-10mm).
  • Borders: Smooth, well-defined, and regular borders.
  • Calcification: The presence of dense, solid white calcification throughout the nodule is a strong indicator of a benign cause.

Cure / Treatment

The standard "treatment" is observation. A doctor will typically recommend a follow-up CT scan in 6-12 months to confirm the nodule is stable and not growing. If it remains unchanged for two years, it is considered definitively benign and no further action is needed.

{% elif class_name == 'malignant' %}

Malignant Cases

General classification for cancerous tumors.

What this classification means

A malignant tumor is **cancerous**. It is made of abnormal cells that grow uncontrollably. If not treated, these tumors can invade nearby tissues and spread (metastasize) through the blood or lymph system to other parts of the body. This is a general category that includes the specific types below (Adenocarcinoma, etc.).

Symptoms

In early stages, lung cancer may cause no symptoms. As it progresses, common symptoms can include:

  • A persistent cough that doesn't go away or gets worse.
  • Coughing up blood (hemoptysis).
  • Chest pain, shortness of breath, or wheezing.
  • Hoarseness or unexplained weight loss.

Diagnosis

A suspicious nodule on a CT scan is the first step. A definitive diagnosis is made with a **biopsy**, where a small sample of the nodule is removed (using a needle or a scope) and examined by a pathologist.

{% elif class_name == 'adeno' %}

Adenocarcinoma

A specific type of malignant cancer.

What it Is

Adenocarcinoma is the **most common type of lung cancer**, especially in non-smokers (though it also occurs in smokers). It starts in the glandular cells on the outer parts of the lungs that produce mucus. It tends to grow more slowly than other types.

Diagnosis

It often appears on a CT scan as a "solitary pulmonary nodule" or a "ground-glass opacity" in the periphery (outer edges) of the lung. A **biopsy** is required for confirmation. Doctors will also perform genetic testing on the biopsy sample to look for specific mutations (like EGFR, ALK, KRAS) that can be treated with specific drugs.

Cure / Treatment

The cure depends on the stage. If caught early (Stage I or II), **surgery (lobectomy)** to remove the tumor can be curative. In more advanced stages (Stage III or IV), treatment involves a combination of **chemotherapy, radiation therapy, and/or immunotherapy**. The most effective treatment for advanced cases is often **targeted therapy**—pills that specifically target the genetic mutations (like EGFR) found during diagnosis.

Large Cell Carcinoma

A specific type of malignant cancer.

What it Is

This is a fast-growing, "undifferentiated" type of non-small cell lung cancer. When viewed under a microscope, the cancer cells are very large and abnormal, lacking the features of other types. It can appear in any part of the lung and tends to spread quickly.

Symptoms

Because it grows quickly, it often causes symptoms related to its size and spread, such as chest pain, shortness of breath, and cough. It can also be associated with "paraneoplastic syndromes" (hormones released by the tumor that cause symptoms in other parts of the body).

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis is made via biopsy. Treatment is aggressive due to its fast growth. If it hasn't spread, **surgery** is an option. More often, **chemotherapy** and **radiation therapy** are the primary treatments. It is less likely to respond to targeted therapies but may respond to **immunotherapy**.

{% elif class_name == 'squamous' %}

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

A specific type of malignant cancer.

What it Is

This type of cancer starts in the squamous cells, which are the thin, flat cells that line the inside of the airways (bronchi) in the lungs. It is **very strongly linked to a history of smoking** and is usually found in the central part of the lungs, near the main airways (bronchial tubes).

Symptoms

Because it grows in the central airways, it often causes symptoms earlier than other types by blocking airflow. These can include a persistent **cough, coughing up blood (hemoptysis)**, wheezing, and recurrent lung infections (pneumonia) in the same spot.

Cure / Treatment

Treatment is based on stage. Early-stage tumors may be treated with **surgery**. For more advanced tumors, **chemotherapy and radiation therapy** (often given together) are very common. In recent years, **immunotherapy** (which boosts the body's own immune system to fight the cancer) has become a highly effective first-line treatment for advanced squamous cell lung cancer.
